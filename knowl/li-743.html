Item 1.5.10.b.
\({11 \choose 4}\text{.}\) This is in fact a stars and bars problem. The stars are the 4 inputs and the bars are the 7 spots between the 8 possible outputs. Think of it this way - we will specify \(f(1)\text{,}\) then \(f(2)\text{,}\) then \(f(3)\text{,}\) and so on in that order. Start with the possible output 0. We can use it as the output of \(f(1)\text{,}\) or we can switch to 1 as a potential output. Say we put \(f(1) = 1\text{.}\) Now we are at 1 (canâ€™t go back to 0). Should \(f(2) = 1\text{?}\) If yes, then we are putting down another star. If no, put down a bar and switch to 2. Maybe you switch to 3, then assign \(f(2) = 3\) and \(f(3) = 3\) (two more stars) before switching to 4 as a possible output. And so on.
